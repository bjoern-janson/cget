# 00 – Overview

# Causal Generative Equilibrium Theory (CGET)

## Purpose

Causal Generative Equilibrium Theory (CGET) is a research framework for studying intelligence as a process of discovering compact, executable models of reality while preserving the structural invariants required for coherent self-modification.

Rather than defining intelligence in terms of prediction, optimization, or search alone, CGET proposes that intelligence emerges from the interaction of three fundamental processes:

1. **Compression** — discovering simpler representations that preserve causal structure.
2. **Execution** — using those representations to efficiently generate and evaluate future possibilities.
3. **Invariant Preservation** — maintaining the structural properties that allow the system to continue improving without destroying its own coherence.

The framework attempts to unify ideas from causal inference, information theory, representation learning, control theory, dynamical systems, and alignment within a single mathematical language.

---

# Central Hypothesis

The central hypothesis of CGET is:

> Intelligence is the capacity of a self-modifying system to discover executable causal representations that expand controllable future reach while preserving the invariants required for coherent transformation.

This shifts the emphasis from "making accurate predictions" to "building representations that remain useful under intervention, composition, and self-improvement."

---

# The Core Problem

An intelligent system faces two coupled problems.

The first is understanding the external world.

The second is maintaining itself while changing.

These problems are treated symmetrically.

## Epistemic Compression

The external world is compressed into an executable causal language,

[
\mathcal{G}=(Z,\mathcal{B},\Omega,\rho),
]

where latent entities, primitive operators, composition rules, and transition dynamics together define a reusable model of reality.

## Normative Compression

The internal value structure is compressed into a viability model,

[
V_{\mathcal O}=V_{\text{core}}\oplus V_{\text{adaptive}},
]

which separates slowly changing identity constraints from rapidly changing instrumental knowledge.

Together these form the coupled state of an intelligent agent.

# Research Themes

The framework is organized around several connected questions.

## Representation

What makes a representation executable rather than descriptive?

## Compression

How can unnecessary degrees of freedom be removed without destroying causal structure?

## Generalization

Why do some abstractions compose while others memorize?

## Agency

How should controllable futures be distinguished from merely reachable futures?

## Alignment

How can self-modifying systems preserve coherent identity while continuously improving?

## Self-Improvement

What mathematical constraints separate productive self-modification from self-corruption?

---

# Guiding Principle

A recurring idea throughout the framework is:

> Remove unnecessary degrees of freedom while preserving invariant degrees of freedom.

This principle appears across many domains:

* symmetry reduction in physics,
* quotient constructions in mathematics,
* latent representations in machine learning,
* causal abstraction in science,
* identity preservation during self-modification.

CGET proposes that these are different manifestations of the same computational operation.

---

# Scope

CGET is not intended to replace existing theories of intelligence.

Instead, it attempts to provide a common mathematical language capable of relating ideas from multiple fields, including:

* Causal inference
* Representation learning
* Information theory
* Algorithmic complexity
* Dynamical systems
* Control theory
* Reinforcement learning
* AI alignment

The framework remains an active research project. Definitions, formalizations, proofs, and empirical validation are expected to evolve as the theory develops.

# Long-Term Goal

The long-term objective of CGET is to develop a falsifiable mathematical framework capable of describing:

* representation,
* reasoning,
* agency,
* alignment,
* and self-modification

within a single coherent formal system.

Whether the framework succeeds depends not on elegance, but on whether it produces useful definitions, testable predictions, and explanatory power beyond existing approaches.
